# Coexistence Systems

Status: Draft

## Introduction

All systems that persist across time exist alongside other agents.

No system operates in isolation. Biological organisms exist within ecosystems, individuals exist within social environments, and institutions exist within broader networks of interaction. These systems are composed of multiple agents whose actions influence not only their own continuation, but also the continuation of others.

To understand how cooperation becomes possible, it is first necessary to understand what exists within such systems.

This chapter examines coexistence systems: the environments in which multiple agents exist, interact, and affect one another under shared continuation constraints.

---

## Coexistence Systems

A coexistence system consists of multiple agents sharing space, resources, and continuation constraints.

An agent is any entity capable of actions that affect continuation, either its own or that of others.

Within a coexistence system:

- agents seek continuation  
- agents interact directly or indirectly  
- agents influence each other’s continuation  

These interactions form the underlying structure from which more complex systems emerge.

---

## Coexistence in Biological Systems

Biological systems provide clear examples of coexistence.

Organisms exist within environments populated by other organisms. These include species that compete for resources, organisms that depend on one another for survival, and entities that derive benefit at the expense of others.

Some interactions enhance continuation. Symbiotic relationships allow multiple organisms to survive more effectively together than alone. Other interactions degrade continuation. Pathogens and parasites sustain themselves by reducing the capacity of their hosts.

In many cases, agents are not externally separate. Some exist within others.

Biological systems frequently contain embedded agents whose effects are mediated through the structure of the host. Microorganisms within a host organism may contribute to energy processes or may degrade system function. These embedded relationships form layered coexistence systems in which agents operate at different levels of separation.

The presence of such agents does not, by itself, determine whether the system is stable. Both beneficial and harmful agents may persist within the same structure.

---

## Coexistence in Physical Systems

Physical systems also exhibit coexistence, though in a different form.

Components within physical systems interact according to deterministic rules. The behaviour of these components is governed by stable relationships, and outcomes can often be predicted with high precision.

In such systems, coexistence does not depend on voluntary participation. Interactions occur according to fixed laws, and the continuation of the system is embedded within its structure.

Because behaviour is predictable, the uncertainty associated with future actions is limited. The effects of interactions are largely determined by the underlying properties of the system.

---

## Coexistence at the Individual Level

At the level of individuals, coexistence becomes more complex.

Individuals operate within environments shaped by other individuals, institutions, and external conditions. They interact through exchange, cooperation, competition, and conflict.

These interactions may take different forms:

- contribution, where actions enhance the continuation of others  
- extraction, where actions reduce the capacity of others  
- competition, where agents pursue continuation under shared constraints  
- parasitic relationships, where one agent benefits at the expense of another  

Individuals may also contain embedded systems. Internal processes, external influences, and environmental conditions may all affect an individual’s capacity for continuation.

The effects of these interactions are not fixed. They vary across time and depend on changing conditions.

---

## Coexistence in Social Systems

Social systems extend coexistence across structured environments.

Families, organizations, and communities consist of individuals whose actions are interdependent. These systems rely on ongoing participation, and their continuation depends on the collective behaviour of their members.

Within social systems, agents are not uniformly aligned. Some actions contribute to shared continuation, while others may disrupt or degrade it.

Participation is not fully determined. Individuals may choose to contribute, withdraw, or act in ways that affect others differently over time.

As a result, social coexistence systems contain a mixture of:

- cooperative agents  
- non-cooperative agents  
- neutral or latent agents whose effects depend on context  

These classifications are not fixed. They depend on the observed impact of agents on continuation within a given system.

---

## Coexistence at the Civilizational Level

At the scale of civilizations, coexistence systems become highly complex.

Large populations of individuals, institutions, and systems interact across extended networks. Economic systems, legal frameworks, and governance structures organize these interactions across time and space.

Civilizations contain a wide range of agents with differing effects on continuation. Some contribute to stability and growth, while others introduce risk, disruption, or degradation.

These agents may operate at different levels:

- individuals within institutions  
- institutions within economies  
- systems embedded within broader systems  

This creates layered coexistence structures in which interactions are mediated both directly and through organizational forms.

The persistence of such systems does not imply that all agents contribute positively to continuation. Agents may remain within the system regardless of their effects.

---

## Continuation Pressure in Coexistence Systems

Coexistence systems naturally generate pressures that affect continuation.

These include:

- capacity constraints, as agents compete or interact under limited resources  
- uncertainty, as future behaviour cannot be fully predicted  
- interaction risk, as agents may affect each other positively or negatively  

These pressures arise because multiple agents operate within shared environments under conditions that are not fully controlled.

Not all agents support system continuation. Some enhance it, while others degrade it.

---

## From Coexistence to Cooperation

Coexistence systems contain agents with differing effects on continuation.

Because not all agents contribute positively, cooperation does not arise automatically from coexistence alone.

Agents may persist within the system regardless of whether they enhance or degrade continuation.

A mechanism is therefore required to determine which agents may participate in cooperative processes and under what conditions.

This leads to the concept of the cooperative boundary, which defines the conditions under which cooperation can be sustained.
