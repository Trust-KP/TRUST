23	APPENDIX – Framework Interpretative Principle
23.1	TOKENISATION PRINCIPLE

When credit claims become transferable across agents,vthey form tokens representing claims on future contribution. The stability of such tokens depends on CREDIT–CONTRIBUTION ATTRIBUTION PRINCIPLE

New contribution generated within the system must be attributable, in whole or in part, to previously extended credit commitments.

Credit represents forward-delegated trust that mobilises resources before the corresponding contribution has been realised.

When production occurs, the resulting contribution resolves, repays, or validates the earlier credit that enabled it.

This creates a temporal linkage between past commitments and future realised outcomes.

Structural attribution condition:

Contribution(t) → resolves → Credit(t−k)

where k represents the temporal lag between credit extension and realised contribution.

If new contribution cannot be reasonably attributed to previous credit commitments, the system experiences credit overextension, where commitments expand faster
than verifiable contribution.

Conversely, when realised contribution persistently exceeds outstanding credit commitments, the system generates surplus credibility and expands its capacity to sustain future credit.

This attribution mechanism ensures that forward commitments remain anchored to realised contribution over time.

23.2	CONTRIBUTION ATTRIBUTION PRINCIPLE

Credit commitments generate expectations of future contribution.

ExpectedContribution(t+k) ← Credit(t)

When realised contribution occurs, the system compares
actual outcomes with these expectations.

ActualContribution(t+k) − ExpectedContribution(t+k) = ContributionVariance

This variance updates credibility assessments and influences future credit allocation.

Contribution resolves prior credit commitments.

23.3	FORWARD CREDIT PRINCIPLE

Agents may issue credit claims against expected future contribution, allowing resources to be mobilised before contribution is realised.

However, sustainable credit expansion requires that the total volume of outstanding credit commitments remains consistent with credible expectations of future contribution.
23.4	Trust Delta Principle
The difference between theoretical trust implied by contribution history and observed trust interpreted from available signals constitutes the trust delta. This delta arises from signal overlap, signal delay, signal distortion, and observer weighting.
23.5	Observer Principle
Trust and credibility are assigned by observers rather than possessed intrinsically by agents.
Agents generate observable contribution signals through their behaviour and outcomes. Observers interpret these signals through different trust lenses to form trust judgements. Credibility therefore reflects the observer’s interpretation of contribution history rather than an inherent property of the agent.
Because observers possess different information sets, priorities, and decision contexts, the same agent may be assigned different credibility assessments by different observers even when the underlying contribution history remains unchanged.
23.6	Balanced Contribution Ledger
 
Institutional coordination under the instrumental application of the Trust Framework is grounded in the Balanced Contribution Principle.
 
Within institutional systems, all coordinated activity is represented through a balanced contribution ledger. Each contribution event must record both:
 
• the creation of value generated by the action, and
• the system load or resource consumption required to produce that value.
 
Accordingly, no institutional action is recorded as a unilateral gain. Every contribution must be accompanied by a corresponding counter-entry reflecting the institutional, infrastructural, ecological, or organisational resources engaged in the activity.
 
This rule extends the structural logic of double-entry accounting beyond monetary transactions to the broader domain of coordinated societal activity. The contribution ledger therefore serves as the observable institutional history of continuation-relevant actions.
 
Within the instrumental domain, institutional interpretation proceeds through the following structure:
 
1.	Contribution Ledger
Verified contribution events are recorded as balanced entries capturing both value creation and system load.
2.	Domain Interpretation
Institutional domains apply context-specific interpretation rules to evaluate the continuation relevance of recorded contributions.
3.	Trust Interpretation
From interpreted contribution history, the system derives trust signals representing continuation capability under uncertainty.
4.	Delegated Authority
Delegation of decision authority within institutions is calibrated according to trust state and admissibility constraints.
5.	Ownership Attribution
Accumulated contribution history determines proportional claims on generated surplus within the institutional domain.
 
The institutional mechanisms described in this Part—including capital allocation, expansion admissibility, risk pooling, ecological load constraints, and public revenue structures—are therefore interpreted as domain-specific applications of the Balanced Contribution Principle.
 
This principle applies exclusively within the instrumental domain of trust. It does not redefine relational, procedural, or symbolic trust modes described in Part I. Those domains operate under distinct epistemic conditions and remain outside the scope of the institutional contribution ledger.

23.7	Signal Structure Principle
Signals within the framework arise in three distinct stages.
Contribution signals originate from observable actions and outcomes produced by agents. These signals represent the direct evidence of behaviour that affects continuation conditions.
Trust signals emerge when observers interpret contribution signals through a trust lens such as relational, symbolic, procedural, or instrumental trust. These signals represent the observer’s judgement about the reliability of future interaction based on past contribution history.
Credibility signals arise when trust signals are aggregated and communicated to other observers. Examples include ratings, certifications, reputation indicators, or expert assessments. In large systems many observers rely on credibility signals rather than directly observing the underlying contribution history.
23.8	Signal Detection Principle
Signals represent observable or interpretable traces of contribution events within the system.
Signals may arise through direct observation, recorded outcomes, or indirect inference from observable effects.
Interpretive lenses do not create signals.
They determine how available signals are interpreted.
A signal may exist even if no observer currently interprets it, although in practice institutional systems can only operate on signals that have been detected or recorded.
23.9	Signal–Driver Principle
Signals may influence the behaviour of agents within the system.
However, signals themselves do not constitute contribution events.
Only the actions taken by agents in response to signals generate new contribution events that become part of contribution history.
Signals therefore function as drivers of behaviour, not as contributions themselves.
23.10	External Mirror Principle

A counterparty’s assessment of credit risk to us provides an externalised signal of how our continuation risk is perceived from outside the institution. Counterparty-assessed credit risk provides an external mirror of institutional continuation risk.

By collecting and attributing these external risk perceptions across stakeholder groups, an institution can estimate which trust lenses are perceived as weak and whether continuation concerns are local, concentrated, or systemic.
23.11	Contribution Recognition Principle
Contribution events may occur without immediate trust formation when the event is not observable or interpretable through available trust lenses.
Trust signals arise only when contribution signals become observable to an interpreter.
Accordingly, contribution history may exist in the underlying ledger even when no trust signal has yet been generated.
Trust therefore reflects recognised contribution, not necessarily all contribution that has occurred.
23.12	Delayed Recognition Principle
Contribution events affecting system continuation may occur without immediate trust adjustment when the effects are not yet observable or attributable.
When such events later become observable, trust signals may adjust abruptly as observers reinterpret past contribution history.
Delayed recognition may therefore produce sudden credibility collapse despite long periods of apparent stability.
23.13	Attribution Principle
Contribution events recorded in the contribution ledger do not inherently specify the responsible agent.

When observers interpret contribution signals, they must infer which agent or agents produced the observed effects. This attribution process forms part of trust interpretation rather than the underlying contribution record.

Because observers operate with incomplete information, attribution may be uncertain, contested, or incorrect.

Trust signals therefore reflect attributed contribution rather than necessarily the true origin of contribution events.

Misattribution may occur when responsibility for a contribution event is assigned to the wrong agent, when responsibility is diffused across multiple agents, or when the true source of the contribution remains unknown.

Such misattribution may cause trust loss or gain to occur independently of the actual contribution history recorded in the ledger
23.13.1	Valuation Principle
Trust interpretation depends on the valuation principles applied by observers or institutions.
A valuation principle determines:
• which signals are considered relevant
• how signals are weighted
• how signals are combined when forming trust or credibility judgements
Different domains may apply different valuation principles depending on their coordination requirements.
Valuation principles therefore govern both the interpretation of signals within a trust lens and the aggregation of signals when forming credibility.

23.14	CREDIT SOCIABILITY PRINCIPLE

An agent is credit-sociable when other participants in the system
are willing to extend credit commitments based on credible expectations
of that agent’s future contribution.

Credit sociability therefore depends on the stability of trust signals,
credibility assessments, and institutional trust mechanisms.Domain Interpretation Principle
 
The Trust Framework does not prescribe a universal quantitative model for interpreting contribution events. Instead, it establishes a common ledger structure and interpretation architecture. Domain-specific lenses may implement their own models, measurement systems, and evaluation rules appropriate to the operational context. These models interpret the shared contribution ledger to derive continuation-relevant trust signals within the domain. The framework therefore specifies the institutional structure of interpretation rather than a single universal model.
23.15	Trust Formation Principle
Trust formation occurs when contribution signals are interpreted within a specific trust lens.
Relational trust arises from repeated interaction and familiarity between agents.
Symbolic trust arises from reputation markers, identity signals, or socially recognised symbols.
Procedural trust arises from compliance with rule-based systems and institutional procedures.
Instrumental trust arises from measurable performance outcomes and observable results.
Each lens interprets contribution signals according to its own rules and evaluation criteria.
23.16	Trust Transformation Principle
Trust transformation occurs when evidence produced within one trust lens is interpreted as input for another trust lens.
For example, instrumental performance may influence relational perception, procedural certification may create symbolic legitimacy, or symbolic reputation may shape relational expectations.
Evidence may circulate between lenses, but the interpretive rules of each lens remain distinct. Transformation transfers evidence across lenses without collapsing them into a single mode of trust.
23.17	Domain Non-Fungibility of Trust
 
Because domain lenses apply different interpretation rules, identical contribution histories may produce different trust states across domains.
 
Therefore trust is domain-specific and non-fungible, and trust accumulated in one domain does not automatically transfer to another.
 
Cross-domain relevance may arise only where interpretation lenses assign positive weight to shared contribution categories.
23.18	Credibility Aggregation Principle
Credibility arises when multiple trust signals are evaluated together within a given decision context.
The method by which signals are combined depends on the valuation principles applied by the observer or institution.
Different systems may therefore aggregate trust signals using different rules, including weighting, threshold conditions, procedural validation, or probabilistic assessment.
The framework does not prescribe a universal aggregation formula.
Aggregation remains domain-specific and determined by the valuation principles governing the decision environment.
23.19	Temporal Interpretation Independence
 
The contribution ledger records events without deletion or decay.
 
Temporal relevance is determined by the interpretation lens rather than the ledger itself.
 
Domains may apply temporal weighting, rolling observation windows, or permanent recognition depending on operational characteristics.
 
Thus, older contributions remain part of the observable record even when a domain chooses not to weight them in its trust interpretation.

23.19.1	Temporal Weighting Principle
Contribution events accumulate irreversibly through time within the underlying contribution history.
Past events cannot be erased from the historical record of interaction.
However, the informational weight assigned to contribution signals may vary across time depending on the valuation principles applied by observers or institutions.
Older signals may lose interpretive relevance as new evidence becomes available, while recent signals may receive greater weight when evaluating expected continuation.
Temporal weighting therefore affects interpretation of signals, not the existence of contribution history.
23.20	Credibility Principle
Credibility represents the aggregation of trust signals across multiple trust lenses.
However, credibility is not a fixed property of an agent.
Observers assign different weights to relational, symbolic, procedural, and instrumental trust depending on the decision context. Different purposes therefore produce different credibility configurations even when the underlying contribution signals remain unchanged.
The same agent may therefore appear credible in one context but not in another because different decision contexts emphasise different trust lenses.
23.21	Credibility systemic risk
Credibility systemic risk arises when multiple trust signals depend on a shared attribution that later proves incorrect.
Because trust signals may appear diversified while relying on the same underlying attribution, correction of the attribution may cause simultaneous credibility collapse across multiple trust modes.
This form of systemic risk arises not from changes in the contribution ledger itself, but from revision of the attribution structure through which observers interpret contribution signals.
23.22	Actual Trust and Projected Trust Principle
Trust evaluation incorporates both realised contribution history and projected continuation capacity.
Actual trust represents the current in-force trust signal derived from observed contribution history.
Projected trust represents the observer’s expectation of future contribution capacity, referred to as Expected Continuation Value (ECV).
Observers therefore form forward-looking expectations about the ability of an agent to sustain future contributions under current conditions. As new contribution signals are realised over time, the difference between projected expectations and realised outcomes leads to revision of trust assessments.
23.23	Trust Update Principle
Changes in trust signals may arise from three distinct sources.
First, data updates occur when new contribution signals are observed. Under fixed interpretation rules, positive contributions increase trust while negative contributions reduce trust.
Second, parameter updates occur when observers adjust the weighting or thresholds used to interpret contribution signals.
Third, model or scope updates occur when the interpretive structure itself changes, for example through new evaluation frameworks or expanded observation domains.
These mechanisms distinguish between updates driven by new evidence and reinterpretation of existing evidence.
23.24	Meta-Framework Principle
The Trust Framework operates as a meta-framework for interpreting contribution, trust, credibility, and continuation under uncertainty.
The framework defines the structural relationships between contribution signals, trust formation lenses, credibility aggregation, and observer interpretation. Domain-specific models represent downstream instantiations of this structure.
Each domain determines which contribution signals are observable, which trust lenses dominate decision making, and how credibility weighting operates in practice. The core framework therefore provides a general interpretive architecture while allowing measurement, modelling, and management practices to be adapted to the requirements of specific institutional contexts.


Principle: Evidence Admissibility & Truth Selection

Statement

In any domain, trust interpretation must be based on appropriately selected evidence and truth type.

Admissible evidence must be consistent with the domain’s coordination requirements, including its level of observability, comparability across agents, and capacity constraints.

Truth must be classified according to whether the domain requires personal or impersonal evaluation, and whether the function is operational (coordination) or normative (judgement).

Misapplication of evidence or truth type leads to systematic misinterpretation of contribution and breakdown in coordination.

⸻

Operational Form
Domain
→ determines admissible evidence
→ determines truth type (personal/impersonal × operational/normative)
→ determines trust interpretation
Corollaries
1. Domain-Dependence
There is no universal evidence standard or single truth form applicable across all domains.
⸻
2. Evidence Constraint
Only evidence that is observable, comparable, and appropriate to the domain’s capacity structure should be used for trust evaluation.
⸻
3. Truth Non-Substitutability
Personal and impersonal truths, and operational and normative truths, are not interchangeable across domains.
⸻
4. Failure Condition
Applying:
	•	intention-based (personal) truth to impersonal coordination domains, or
	•	outcome-based (impersonal) truth to capacity-constrained relational domains
leads to coordination failure or structural injustice.

⸻
Compressed Form
Correct domain → correct evidence → correct truth type → valid trust
Otherwise → misinterpretation → coordination breakdown
